So you want to be a lawyer, huh?

By Peter Yang, posted May 27, 2017

Introduction

As an experienced lawyer, I am often asked about the profession from all walks of life, from young mothers to parents with children studying law. In today's publication, I will answer some of these questions.

Is it possible to continue to practice law if, as a lawyer, you have been convicted of a felony?
While conviction of a felony does not automatically disqualify a lawyer from practicing, conviction of most felonies and some misdemeanors will result in disbarment or sometimes lesser discipline like suspension. 
 
The conviction of certain crimes involving moral turpitude will result in the automatic revocation of a lawyer's license to practice law. Moral turpitude has no precise definition, but includes theft, fraud, forgery, deceit and the making of false statements.
 

What consequences may an attorney face if caught committing a crime?
Lawyers must adhere to the standards of professional conduct that exist in the province in which they practice. Each province has a disciplinary system that allows lawyers to be punished for breaching ethical standards, including subjecting them to a professional discipline.
 
Discipline of any lawyer is a serious matter, because it can lead to the permanent loss of the lawyer's license to practice in the province. As with regular criminal defendants, lawyers entering the disciplinary system are afforded rights to ensure fair treatment. A thorough investigation of the case is conducted, and the lawyer is given the opportunity to present a defense. 


What should a criminal defense attorney do if he knows his client is guilty?
Criminal defense attorneys are ethically obliged to zealously represent all their clients, the guilty as well as the innocent. Moreover, no matter what the defendant has done, he is not legally guilty until a prosecutor offers enough evidence to persuade a judge or jury to convict. And oftentimes, criminal defense attorneys are almost never truly sure whether the defendant is guilty of the crime he or she has been charged with. Just because the defendant says he did it doesn't make it so. The defendant may be lying to take the rap for someone he wants to protect, or may be guilty, but guilty of a different and lesser crime than the one being prosecuted. For these reasons, among others, many defense lawyers never ask their clients if they committed the crime. Instead, the lawyer uses the facts to put on the best defense possible and leaves the question of guilt to the judge or jury.


Must one become an attorney before becoming a judge? If so, for how long?
In most cases, judges are attorneys who have several years of experience practicing law. To become an attorney, one must complete a three-year Juris Doctor program after earning a bachelor’s degree. After completing law school, JD graduates must pass their province’s bar exam, an accomplishment that entails months of preparation and study.

That said, depending on where one lives, a law degree or experience working as a lawyer is not always required to become a judge. For example, the United States Constitution contains no prerequisites for appointment to the Supreme Court.
 

What are the disadvantages of being an attorney?
A career as a lawyer is one of the most sought-after professions in the world. Indeed, working as a lawyer has many advantages. However, alongside these wonderful benefits are major disadvantages. In the slide show below, you will find five disadvantages that all potential lawyers should consider.

High stress

Deadlines, billing pressures, client demands, long hours, changing laws and other demands all combine to make the practice of law one of the most stressful jobs on the planet. Throw in rising business pressures, evolving legal technologies, and climbing law school debt, and it’s no wonder that lawyers are stressed.

Long hours

Rising workloads and shrinking staff translate into more work hours for lawyers. Today’s lawyers work longer and harder than ever and 50+ hour work weeks are not uncommon. In addition to billing hours, today’s competitive environment has forced lawyers to spend more time on client development and business management activities. Thus, many lawyers complain of a lack of work-life balance.

Professional
dissatisfaction

The stress and demands of law practice have fueled high levels of career dissatisfaction among lawyers. With depression and suicide being common occurrences, forty-four percent of lawyers recently surveyed by the American Bar Association said they would not recommend the profession to a young person.

Law school debt

The cost of a law school education has outpaced inflation in recent years. Tuition at even mediocre law schools can cost up to $43,000 a year and six-figure law school debt is not uncommon. In today’s cut-throat job market, new graduates often do not earn enough to repay law school debt and a law degree is no longer a ticket to financial security.

Competitive job market

Job openings for lawyers have plunged, but law schools are not dialing back enrollment. Increased competitive pressures have forced many lawyers to settle for less-than-ideal employment or change careers altogether. Today’s lawyers face one of the bleakest job markets in history; record numbers of jobs have been cut and salaries have plummeted. A steady supply of lawyers coupled with declining demand has caused many legal professionals to rethink the value of a law degree.
